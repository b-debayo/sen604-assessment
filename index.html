1Overview

Proloquo2Go is a symbol-based augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) application developed by AssistiveWare, an Amsterdam-based company founded in 2000 whose products are used in over 150 countries (AssistiveWare, 2024). The app provides a grid of picture symbols that generate spoken output when selected, allowing children and young people who have limited spoken language to make requests, comments, questions, and extended contributions in classroom, home, and community settings. It is widely used in the United Kingdom, the United States, Australia, and parts of Europe, and is one of the most-cited mainstream AAC products in the peer-reviewed literature (Beukelman and Light, 2020).

The application is not a communication miracle. Its value sits in a particular design choice, namely the Crescendo vocabulary, which prioritises core words such as want, go, more, help, and stop across every page, with topic-specific fringe vocabulary nested underneath. Light and McNaughton (2014) argue that core vocabulary should sit at the heart of any AAC system because around eighty per cent of utterances in everyday speech use a small bank of high-frequency words. A design that hides those words behind category folders forces children to learn the system rather than learn to communicate, which is a frequent reason for AAC abandonment (Beukelman and Light, 2020).

This wiki page sets out what Proloquo2Go offers, where the evidence base is strong and where it is weaker, and how UK SEND practitioners can plan implementation that avoids the common pitfalls. It draws on a project log for a Year 3 pupil referred to as Jamie, who has autism spectrum condition and developmental language disorder, to ground abstract claims in a concrete case. Pseudonyms and summarised context are used throughout, consistent with the British Educational Research Association (2024) guidelines.

2Key features and functionality

Proloquo2Go combines five functional elements that are worth distinguishing, because schools often evaluate the app by its surface appearance rather than by how these elements interact.

2.1Crescendo core vocabulary

The Crescendo vocabulary is layered so that a beginner can start with a fifteen-word grid and progress, without being relearned, through intermediate and advanced layouts that share the same word positions. Motor-planning consistency matters because children with communication difficulties often rely on muscle memory to locate words quickly (Beukelman and Light, 2020). A vocabulary that moves words between versions undermines that memory; a stable vocabulary preserves it.

2.2Symbol system and personalisation

Symbols are drawn from the SymbolStix set, which offers diverse representations of age, ability, and ethnicity. Practitioners can replace any symbol with a photograph, a drawing, or a Creative Commons image, which is especially useful for names, local places, and culturally specific foods. This matters for equity. As Lynch, Singal and Francis (2022) argue, AAC systems imported without cultural adaptation frequently fail in low and middle-income contexts, and the same logic applies at classroom level within the United Kingdom.

2.3Speech output and language settings

The app generates speech through a selection of voices in over twenty languages, including British English, Spanish, Dutch, and Arabic. Children can keep their spoken identity across contexts, for example by using a child voice at home and a school-age voice with peers. Bilingual households can set a primary language and add a secondary language without building two separate systems.

2.4Progress monitoring and data

Proloquo2Go logs the words a user selects, which allows staff and families to review vocabulary growth over time, identify over-reliance on a narrow core, and target intervention. Used well, this feature supports the evidence-informed planning called for by Edyburn (2015). Used poorly, it can turn AAC into a surveillance exercise that makes the child feel monitored rather than listened to, so data access should be agreed with the family from the outset.

2.5Integration with writing and literacy tools

Sentences composed in Proloquo2Go can be shared, copied, or dictated into other apps. When paired with Clicker 8 (Crick Software, 2024), children can move from speaking a sentence to selecting the same words on a writing grid, which helps bridge oral and written language. This cross-modal function is arguably the most underused feature in UK classrooms, and the one with the strongest implications for curriculum access (Department for Education, 2020a).

3Supporting children with SEND

The case for Proloquo2Go in SEND provision is not primarily technological. It is educational and relational. Three groups of children and young people are commonly supported well by the tool, each with a different reason.

  • Children with autism spectrum condition and complex communication needs, for whom symbol-based systems can reduce the processing demand of producing speech under time pressure (Logan, Iacono and Trembath, 2017).
  • Children with developmental language disorder, apraxia, or dysarthria, who understand far more than they can say and benefit from a reliable output channel while oral language develops.
  • Children with Down syndrome, cerebral palsy, and acquired neurological conditions, for whom AAC is often the main route into curriculum participation (Beukelman and Light, 2020).

Framing AAC as a support for complex communication needs rather than a replacement for speech is important. Millar, Light and Schlosser's (2006) widely cited meta-analysis found that AAC use is associated with modest gains rather than losses in spoken output, contradicting a common fear that devices displace talking. This finding is not a licence for complacency; partners still need to model spoken alongside aided communication. It is, however, a robust counter to the scepticism that can delay provision by months or years.

3.1Case application: a Year 3 pupil with ASC

Jamie is a seven-year-old with autism spectrum condition and co-occurring developmental language disorder, attending a mainstream primary school in the East Midlands. He is curious, socially motivated, and a strong non-verbal reasoner, but he initiated fewer than two verbal exchanges per hour in Year 3 English and mathematics lessons before AAC provision. The project log for Jamie examined three candidate tools and selected Proloquo2Go because it combined core-word design, a mainstream iPad form factor that reduced stigma, and integration with Clicker 8 for writing. A four-week phased implementation moved from free exploration, through aided language stimulation by adults, into structured opportunities in snack time, guided reading, and computing. Early indicators were encouraging. Jamie's total communicative acts rose, and he produced a four-word spoken sentence during the initial demonstration session that his mother described as the longest she had heard unprompted in weeks. The case illustrates the wider point that AAC success depends less on product features and more on partnership and practice.

Voices from the project

Jamie's mother observed that school meetings can feel like a courtroom, so sitting at a kitchen table changed who spoke first. Jamie's father, initially sceptical, agreed to set a shared measure that Jamie's total communicative acts should rise within six weeks, which turned an ideological concern into a testable proposition.

4Strengths and benefits

The strengths of Proloquo2Go can be grouped under five headings, each connected to a specific need that SEND children and their families report.

4.1Dignity and low stigma

Running AAC on an iPad rather than on a dedicated speech-generating device reduces the visual difference between the child and peers. This matters. Teenagers and older children regularly abandon dedicated devices because of peer comments, a pattern documented in the Department for Education's (2020a) stakeholder report. A mainstream device that also plays games, takes photographs, and reads stories keeps AAC socially ordinary.

4.2Cost sustainability

The one-off purchase model is unusual in an AAC market dominated by subscriptions and high-cost dedicated hardware. Schools operating on constrained SEND budgets can plan three-year costs with reasonable certainty. For families who self-fund, the price point sits within reach of personal budgets under Education, Health and Care Plans (Department for Education and Department of Health, 2015).

4.3Evidence-informed vocabulary

Crescendo's core-word design is not a marketing claim; it reflects decades of AAC research on what makes systems learnable (Light and McNaughton, 2014). The stable motor plan across levels means that words learned at age five can still be found in the same place at age twelve, which protects against the relearning burden that plagues competitor systems when children progress.

4.4Partner-friendly modelling

The simplicity of the grid makes aided language stimulation, in which the partner speaks while touching symbols, accessible to teaching assistants, parents, and siblings without specialist training. Sennott, Light and McNaughton (2016) identify modelling as the single most underused AAC technique, and a device that invites modelling rather than hides it is a practical advantage.

4.5Cross-modal bridge to literacy

Integration with Clicker 8 supports the transition from aided speaking to aided writing, addressing the common gap in SEND provision between communication and curriculum access. The Department for Education's (2020b) publicity on high-tech products to level the playing field emphasises exactly this integration, though in practice schools rarely commission both tools together.

5Barriers to implementation

A balanced wiki page names the barriers as directly as the benefits. Five recurring barriers are worth flagging for any setting considering Proloquo2Go.

5.1Workforce readiness

The most persistent barrier is not the device but the adults around it. The Department for Education's Assistive Technology programme (2020b) identified gaps in staff confidence and training as the primary reason AAC under-performs in schools. Two or three hours of initial training is rarely enough; schools need ongoing coaching, ideally from a speech and language therapist, and protected time for teaching assistants to plan modelling opportunities.

5.2Attentional competition on mainstream devices

An iPad is a communication device in one app and a game console in another. Without structural limits such as iOS Guided Access or Screen Time restrictions, children drift to non-communication uses, which erodes the tool's credibility with adults. This is not an argument against mainstream devices; it is an argument for explicit planning around restrictions, visual timers, and clear transitions.

5.3Platform lock-in

Proloquo2Go runs only on iPad and iPhone. Households using Android or Chromebooks face either a significant additional hardware cost or a cross-platform mismatch with school. This is a real equity issue, since iPads are among the more expensive tablet options, and the cost falls hardest on families already navigating the financial pressure of raising a disabled child (Department for Education, 2020a).

5.4Cultural and linguistic adaptation

While the app supports multiple languages and allows symbol substitution, the default symbol library remains anchored in Western visual conventions. Practitioners working with children from Black, Asian, and minority ethnic backgrounds frequently need to invest time building custom symbols for foods, dress, and family structures (Lynch, Singal and Francis, 2022). Without that investment, the symbol set can quietly convey that the child's world is outside the norm.

5.5Risk of professional over-reliance

A subtler barrier is the professional temptation to treat the app as a complete intervention. Edyburn (2015) warns against this pattern; AAC without partnership training, goal setting, and review becomes a product handed over rather than a system implemented. Wikis and supplier websites rarely state this clearly, which is one reason why an evidence-led page that does so is a useful counterweight.

Key point

The biggest barriers to Proloquo2Go success in the UK are not features of the app. They are gaps in staff training, the absence of protected planning time, and a tendency to treat a device as a complete intervention. A school that buys the app without resourcing the adults around it should expect limited return.

6Impacts on the child's development

The literature on Proloquo2Go's impact can be organised around four developmental domains. Claims in each domain are reported with their strength of evidence, because marketing language that conflates plausibility with proof does children a disservice.

6.1Expressive communication

The strongest evidence base concerns expressive communication. Logan, Iacono and Trembath's (2017) systematic review found moderate evidence for gains in requesting, commenting, and answering in children with autism spectrum condition using symbol-based AAC, with larger effect sizes where aided language stimulation was part of the implementation. The review also flagged that most studies were small and heterogeneous, so effect estimates should be treated as indicative rather than precise.

6.2Spoken language

The fear that AAC delays speech is widespread but not supported by the evidence. Millar, Light and Schlosser's (2006) meta-analysis reported modest increases in spoken output alongside AAC use, a finding that has been replicated in subsequent studies. For children such as Jamie, this suggests that parallel work on spoken and aided communication is the appropriate model, rather than a sequence in which AAC is delayed until spoken language has been given a final chance.

6.3Social participation and wellbeing

Qualitative studies consistently report that successful AAC use shifts children's social position. Parents describe children initiating rather than waiting, taking a turn rather than being skipped, and, over time, beginning to tease or joke using the device (Beukelman and Light, 2020). Roulstone et al.'s (2012) commissioned review of outcomes preferred by children and parents emphasises participation and independence over technical speech measures, which aligns well with what families actually ask for.

6.4Emerging literacy and curriculum access

Evidence on literacy transfer is smaller but growing. Studies linking AAC vocabulary to written word banks report stronger gains when the vocabulary in both systems is aligned, which is the logic behind the Proloquo2Go and Clicker 8 pairing (Crick Software, 2024). For children with developmental language disorder in particular, reliable access to written output can unlock curriculum areas previously inaccessible, including science writing and extended composition.

6.5Ethical and relational impact

Less often named, but arguably as important, is the impact on relationships. Families repeatedly report that hearing their child say I love you or no, thank you through a device changes how the adults in the room talk to and about the child. Erdem (2017) notes that AT effects are frequently felt in altered interaction patterns rather than in measured skill gains. Wiki pages that list only skill outcomes miss this, which is why it warrants an explicit heading here.

7Implementation guidance

Drawing together the project log experience with published guidance, the following checklist summarises a reasonable implementation pathway for UK SEND settings. It is written for a SENCO, class teacher, or parent planning first-time AAC adoption.

  • Establish needs and goals with the child and family first, before shortlisting any product. Use observation across at least two settings.
  • Shortlist two or three AAC options against a weighted matrix including evidence, cost, platform, family preference, and cross-modal fit.
  • Arrange a joint demonstration session at home, with the child exploring the shortlisted apps in an unstructured way. Let the child's engagement guide the choice.
  • Agree a clear, shared success criterion with the family before purchase. For example, total communicative acts rising within six weeks.
  • Plan at least two hours of practice-based staff training on aided language stimulation, not on app mechanics. Book a follow-up session for week four.
  • Set iOS restrictions and transitions to protect the device's communication role. Review restrictions monthly rather than leaving them in place by default.
  • Share a simple weekly log with parents. Keep it short enough to complete on a phone during a commute. Expect partial returns and treat them as useful rather than insufficient.
  • Schedule a formal review at six weeks and at end of term, explicitly inviting family evidence alongside professional data.
  • Align vocabulary across home and school. Agree a single weekly core-word focus that all partners can reinforce.
  • Plan a second charged device as a backup, and a named adult responsible for daily charging.
